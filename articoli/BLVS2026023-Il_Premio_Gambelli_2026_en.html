The “Premio Gambelli” 2026: what it is and what it really measures

The “Premio Gambelli” 2026 was created to reward “gambellianità”

The “Giulio Gambelli” Award is promoted by A.S.E.T. Toscana and was created to honor the memory of Giulio Gambelli, a master taster considered a benchmark for great Tuscan reds. The heart of the award is a precise concept: “gambellianità”, defined as the utmost respect for raw material and the ability to clearly bring out grape variety, territory, and vintage.

This is a practical detail, not a slogan: the rules specify that the jury evaluates the winemaker based on wines produced in Italy and submitted for selection, favoring still and dry wines (in line with Gambelli’s production history). The winner receives a plaque and a financial award.

In short: this is not an “image” award. It is an award that aims to recognize a style of cellar work, measured through the wines and their consistency.

The “Premio Gambelli” 2026 at Cafaggio: Winemaker Francesco Baldacci

Winemaker Francesco Baldacci: background and role at Cafaggio

Francesco Baldacci was born in Florence in 1992 and graduated in Viticulture and Oenology from the University of Florence in 2016. Since 2019, he has been cellar manager at Cafaggio. The award motivation highlights his ability to understand and interpret the Tuscan territory.

In statements reported by WineNews, Baldacci links the award to his work with Sangiovese in Panzano in Chianti and thanks the Cafaggio team. He also mentions the collaboration with Attilio Pagli, described as a pupil of Gambelli, with whom he works to seek the maximum identity of Sangiovese in the area.

The award ceremony for the “Premio Gambelli” 2026 took place in Montepulciano. This is a detail that helps explain the importance of the recognition: a Tuscan context, with consortia and wine-sector professionals involved.

The “Premio Gambelli” 2026 and Cafaggio: why the link with Panzano is central

Cafaggio: an estate in Panzano in the Conca d’Oro

To understand why Cafaggio often returns in conversations about Chianti Classico, you only need to look at its location: the estate is in Panzano in the so-called Conca d’Oro, one of the most cited areas when talking about Sangiovese in its most “precise” expression. The brand page on Vinoso.shop also reconstructs the history of the property, with references going back to 1408.

On the agricultural side, the same page indicates a total area of 60 hectares, with 30 hectares of vineyards and 3 hectares of olive groves, as well as woodland. This is useful information because it helps contextualize the production scale: we are not talking about an unknown “micro-lot,” but about a structured estate that can work consistently on vineyards and selections.

In this framework, Baldacci’s award becomes easier to read: identity is not born from an abstract idea, but from a precise territory and repeated work across vintages.
