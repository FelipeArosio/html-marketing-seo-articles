Sekt and Mosel Riesling: traditional method, perlage and ageing

The traditional method involves a second fermentation in the bottle: this is where pressure and bubbles develop. Wines of Germany explains that Winzersekt matures on the lees for at least 9 months; the VDP, for its own Sekt, applies stricter rules and requires at least 24 months on the lees for vintage wines.

In the glass, time on the lees can add brioche nuances and a more integrated mousse. Consumers do not need to memorize technical rules: it is enough to connect method, perlage and aromas. When a product sheet mentions apple, citrus, smoke and brioche, it is providing concrete clues about the bottle’s style.

FAQ about Sekt and Mosel Riesling

What is Sekt?

Sekt is German sparkling wine. It can be produced using different methods; when the product sheet indicates traditional method or traditional bottle fermentation, the second fermentation takes place in the bottle and generates pressure and bubbles.

What is sparkling wine called in Germany?

In Germany, sparkling wine is called Sekt. In some cases, the term Winzersekt is also found, used to indicate sparkling wines produced by the winegrower, often with greater attention to origin, grape variety and method.

Where is Sekt produced?

Sekt is produced in several German wine regions. In this article, the focus is on the Mosel, because Günther Steinmetz Sekt Brut Riesling Alte Reben is made from Riesling and is connected to the area of Brauneberg.
