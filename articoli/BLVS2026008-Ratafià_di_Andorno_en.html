What Ratafià di Andorno is—and why it’s called a “ratafia” liqueur

Ratafià di Andorno: a simple definition, without confusion with other liqueurs

The name “ratafia” refers to a family of liqueurs made through cold infusions of fruit or flowers in alcohol (in some traditions, also with the addition of wine or spirits). The Piedmontese version is among the best known, and the historic production in Andorno Micca is documented as far back as the 1600s.

In the VINOSO.Shop catalog, the label “Ratafià di Andorno” identifies products from the Rapa Giovanni distillery, active since 1880 in Valle Cervo, near Andorno Micca. If you want to explore the context and more recent history, you can find a dedicated piece in renewed tradition.

What “ratafià” means in Piedmontese: what we actually know

There are two main lines of interpretation behind the name: a popular explanation linking it to the Latin “(ut/pax) rata fiat,” a formula used to ratify an agreement with a toast; and a local Piedmontese reading “rata fià,” often rendered as “gratta fiato.” The latter appears in various popular sources, but it is not unanimously documented.

How Ratafià di Andorno is made: ingredients, profile, and aging

Black cherry and spices: what you’ll find in the glass (example: Ratafià di Andorno Black Cherries)

A concrete reference is Ratafià di Andorno Black Cherries: selected black cherry juice, sugar, and flavorings, with spices such as cinnamon, cloves, and nutmeg left to macerate for a long aging period in stainless-steel containers. The result is a fragrant, easy-drinking liqueur designed to be enjoyed neat or over ice.

If you’re interested in a deeper dive into cherry liqueur (methods and preparation logic), it’s a useful reference for understanding why certain aromas feel so “gentle” on the palate.

ABV and “drinkability”: why balance matters more than the number

The Black Cherry Liqueur is listed at 26% ABV: a strength that supports aroma and sweetness without turning the sip into a show of force. Other versions (such as Walnut Ratafià) may have a higher alcohol content, but the key remains the balance between sweetness, spice, and length.

How to drink Ratafià di Andorno: serving, temperature, and glassware

Chilled, on ice, or “smooth”: three practical ways

To highlight Ratafià di Andorno, keep the service simple: chilled from the fridge for a tighter sip; with a single ice cube to round it out; or neat (“smooth”) if you want to perceive the spices more clearly. A liqueur glass or a small tumbler is perfect—the goal is to concentrate aromas without warming the liqueur too much.

Ratafia liqueur in cocktails: quick ideas, without overcomplicating

You can also use Ratafià in mixing: it works best if you treat it as an aromatic ingredient. Try it in a highball with soda and a citrus peel, or as a “twist” in a light gin and tonic (just a few drops, so you don’t cover up the juniper).

Concrete pairings: Ratafià di Andorno at the table and after dinner

Desserts and fruit: where the “gentle liqueur” shines

Ratafià di Andorno was born for the end of the meal, and it proves it alongside simple desserts: vanilla ice cream, panna cotta, red-fruit tarts, or a fruit salad where you pour in a spoonful of liqueur right before serving.

Cheese: a pairing that surprises

If you prefer savory pairings, go for medium-aged cheeses: the sweetness of ratafia liqueur softens saltiness and brings out the spicy notes. With Walnut Ratafià, the match with bold cheeses can be especially successful.
