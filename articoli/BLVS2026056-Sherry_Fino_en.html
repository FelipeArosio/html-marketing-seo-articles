Sherry Fino and La Ina: flor, fortification and Solera

The most important technical point is the veil of flor. After fermentation, the base wine intended for Fino is fortified to around 15% vol: this environment allows yeasts to form a natural layer on the surface. Flor protects the wine from oxidation and contributes to a drier, fresher and sharper profile.

Why the Solera system makes La Ina recognisable

La Ina ages for 5 years under flor in American oak casks. The Solera y Criaderas system progressively blends wines of different ages, maintaining consistency in style. For the consumer, this explains why the glass is not only dry: it also has breadth, a rounded finish and a more complex sensation than a simple white wine.

Sherry Fino and La Ina: 15% vol and what low alcohol means

When talking about “low alcohol” for Sherry Fino, misunderstandings must be avoided. La Ina is not low alcohol compared with a common still white wine; it is closer to the lower threshold of dry fortified Sherries. Oloroso, for example, is fortified to around 17–18% vol and ages oxidatively, without flor.

Alcohol level and intensity are not the same thing

A wine at 15% vol can feel agile if it is very dry, served chilled and paired with the right dish. The recommended temperature between 7 and 9 °C helps La Ina maintain tension and precision. In practice, alcohol content should be read together with style, service and pairing, not as an isolated figure.

Sherry Fino and La Ina: difference from Oloroso and Pedro Ximénez

The most useful comparison for the reader is between Fino, Oloroso and Pedro Ximénez. Fino is a very dry white Sherry, made from Palomino grapes and aged biologically under flor, a veil of yeasts that protects it from oxidation: the result is a pale, fresh, saline wine with notes of yeast and hazelnut. Oloroso also comes from Palomino, but it does not age under flor: it is fortified to around 17–18% vol and ages in contact with oxygen, developing an amber colour, a richer structure and aromas of hazelnut, almond, dried fruit and spices. Pedro Ximénez, on the other hand, is a very sweet liqueur-like Sherry, produced from sun-dried PX grapes, dark and dense, with aromas of raisins, honey, caramel and coffee.

FAQ on Sherry Fino and La Ina

What is the difference between a Sherry Fino and a Sherry Oloroso?

Sherry Fino is a dry white wine made from Palomino, fortified to around 15% vol and biologically aged under flor. Oloroso also comes from Palomino, but it does not maintain the veil of flor: it is fortified to around 17% vol and ages oxidatively, developing a darker colour, greater body and a more intense profile.

How is Sherry Fino made?

It starts from a dry base wine, usually from Palomino grapes. After fermentation, the wine suitable for biological ageing is fortified to around 15% vol. Flor forms in the cask, a layer of yeasts that protects the wine from oxidation and contributes to its dry, fresh and sharp character. The process continues in the Solera y Criaderas system.

How many types of Sherry are there?

The Consejo Regulador distinguishes three large families: Vinos Generosos, such as Manzanilla, Fino, Amontillado, Oloroso and Palo Cortado; Vinos Generosos de Licor, such as Pale Cream, Medium and Cream; and Vinos Dulces Naturales, such as Moscatel and Pedro Ximénez. To buy well, it is always important to read the type on the label.
