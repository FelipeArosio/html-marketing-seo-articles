What Is Traditionally Eaten on Epiphany: Realistic Ideas for January 6 Dinner

What Is Eaten on Epiphany in Italy: Befana Sweets and Regional Specialties

When people talk about Epiphany, the most recognizable element is often the stocking: sweets, dried fruit, and sweet “coal.” Many regions also have traditional desserts, such as the Piedmontese Fugassa d’la Befana or local biscuits, served at the end of the meal or for breakfast on January 6.

If your January 5 dinner is informal, these sweets can be the perfect ending without the need for elaborate desserts.

January 6 Dinner: When Simple and Leftover-Based Dishes Win

After Christmas and New Year’s Eve, balance becomes a priority: soups, lighter baked pasta, vegetables, and meat or fish dishes without heavy sauces. Leftovers often return to the table, reworked in a simpler, cleaner way.

This variable menu is exactly why the wine for Epiphany dinner should be flexible: it’s best to avoid overly dominant labels and choose wines that remain pleasant through the entire meal.

Wine for Epiphany Dinner: How to Choose Without Mistakes (Even If the Menu Changes)

Wine for Epiphany Dinner and the Mood of the Evening: Family, Friends, or Guests

At home with family, drinkability matters most: a wine that flows easily and doesn’t tire the palate. At friends’ houses, the bottle should also make a good impression—clear style, clean profile, and easy to explain in a couple of sentences.

Wine for Epiphany Dinner: One Bottle or Two? The “Moments” Rule

A practical approach is to think in moments. If there’s no aperitif, one bottle may be enough. If you start with snacks, add a dry sparkling wine or a fresh white, then move on to the dinner wine.

On Epiphany, overall coherence matters more than textbook-perfect pairings.

Wine for Epiphany Dinner by Menu Type: Quick and Useful Pairings

Wine for Epiphany Dinner with First Courses

With comforting pastas and risottos, choose a fresh white or a medium-bodied red depending on the sauce. Creamy or cheese-based dishes call for structure, but without heaviness.

Wine for Epiphany Dinner with Meat or Fish

With fish and vegetables, linear, savory whites work well. With roasted meats or richer dishes, opt for reds that aren’t overly alcoholic, with good acidity to keep the drink lively.

Wine for Epiphany Dinner with Befana Sweets

With candies, biscuits, and sweet coal, a very dry wine may feel rigid. If you stick to one bottle, a dry sparkling wine can still carry through the end. If the dessert is very sweet, consider a second, softer bottle served in small pours.

What Does the Befana Drink? A Playful Way to Choose Wine for Epiphany Dinner

What the Befana Drinks: Between Legend and Hospitality

In Italian folklore, the Befana brings sweets and coal on Epiphany night. There is no “official” wine, but sharing remains central. A welcome glass or a fragrant table wine becomes a gesture of hospitality.

Wine for Epiphany Dinner: A Light Toast to Close the Holidays

Open the evening with a simple toast: dry sparkling wine, served at the right temperature, without excess. It’s an elegant way to say “let’s close the holidays well” and move into a relaxed dinner.

What is traditionally eaten on Epiphany? ▶

What does the Befana drink? ▶

Which wine should you buy to make a good impression? ▶

To make a good impression, choose a wine with a clear, approachable style: traditional-method sparkling wine to start, or a balanced bottle for the table depending on the menu. Serving it at the right temperature and with proper glassware makes the choice feel even more professional, even at home.
